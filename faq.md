# ❓ Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about the Taiwan & Japan family trip (March 28 – April 4, 2026).

---

## 🚇 Getting Around

**Do we need IC cards for transit?**
Yes — get one per person. In Tokyo, use **Suica** (or load it on Apple Wallet). In Osaka, use **ICOCA**. Both work across trains, buses, convenience stores, and vending machines. Tap in, tap out. Children ages 6–11 qualify for half-price child IC cards — buy at a staffed ticket window with proof of age.

**How does the Shinkansen (bullet train) work for our group?**
Book reserved seats in advance on the [JR booking site](https://www.jrpass.com) or at any JR ticket office. Sit together by reserving a row of seats. The Tokyo → Osaka leg takes ~2.5 hours on the Nozomi (non-JR Pass) or ~3 hours on the Hikari (JR Pass eligible). Don't eat or drink loudly — it's fine to eat a bento box quietly.

**Is Google Maps reliable for transit in Japan?**
Yes — Google Maps works excellently for train and subway navigation throughout Tokyo and Osaka, including transfer instructions and platform numbers.

## 💴 Money & Payments

**How much cash should we carry each day?**
Budget at least **¥10,000–20,000 ($65–130 USD) per day** for the whole group. Many smaller restaurants, ramen shops, temples, and street food stalls are cash-only.

**Where do we get cash in Japan?**
**7-Eleven ATMs (7Bank)** and **Japan Post ATMs** reliably accept U.S. debit and credit cards. They're the most foreigner-friendly option. Avoid airport exchange booths — rates are poor.

**Do restaurants and shops accept credit cards?**
Major chains, department stores, and larger hotels yes — but don't rely on it. Small restaurants, ramen shops, night markets, and temple entry fees are often cash-only. Always have yen on hand.

**Should we exchange money before leaving the U.S.?**
You can, but ATM withdrawals in Japan typically give better rates. Check with your bank about foreign ATM fees, or use a fee-free card like Charles Schwab.

## 🗣️ Language

**Do we need to know Japanese to get around?**
No — major tourist areas have English signage, train announcements are bilingual, and many younger locals speak some English. That said, learning a few key phrases is deeply appreciated.

| English | Japanese | Pronunciation |
|---------|----------|---------------|
| Thank you | ありがとうございます | A-ri-ga-tō go-zai-mas |
| Excuse me | すみません | Su-mi-ma-sen |
| How much? | いくらですか？ | I-ku-ra des-ka? |
| Check please | お会計お願いします | O-kai-kei o-ne-gai shi-mas |
| I don't understand | わかりません | Wa-ka-ri-ma-sen |

**What about restaurant menus in Japanese only?**
Use **Google Translate camera mode** — it overlays translations in real time. Many restaurants also have picture menus or plastic food models in the window. Pointing is perfectly acceptable.

## 👧👦 Activities for Kids (Ages 10 & 13)

**What are the best kid-friendly activities in Tokyo?**
- **teamLab Borderless or Planets** — immersive digital art; endlessly fascinating for both ages
- **Akihabara** — electronics, anime, and gaming; perfect for the 13-year-old
- **Asakusa & Senso-ji** — the incense burner and temple grounds are hands-on and memorable
- **Odaiba / teamLab Planets** — Gundam statue, giant Ferris wheel, waterfront views
- **Shibuya Crossing** — the scramble is a spectacle the kids will love

**What are the best kid-friendly activities in Osaka?**
- **Osaka Aquarium Kaiyukan** — one of the largest in the world; whale sharks!
- **Universal Studios Japan** — full theme park; book tickets in advance
- **Dotonbori** — running crab signs, giant octopus balls, street food at every turn
- **Osaka Castle** — history and great views; park space to run around

**Will the kids enjoy the Kyoto day trip?**
Yes — Fushimi Inari's thousands of torii gates are iconic and the hike suits all ages. Arashiyama bamboo grove is quick and photogenic. Nishiki Market has snacks and novelty foods kids enjoy.

## 🌤️ Weather & Packing

**What's the weather like in late March / early April?**
Expect cool to mild temperatures:
- **Tokyo (March 30 – April 2):** 8–17°C (46–63°F), mix of sunny and rainy days
- **Osaka (April 2–4):** 10–19°C (50–66°F), similar conditions

Layers are key. A light jacket, a mid-layer, and a waterproof shell cover most scenarios.

**What should we pack that we might forget?**

| Item | Why |
|------|-----|
| Small towel/handkerchief | Many Japanese restrooms have no paper towels |
| Coin purse | You'll accumulate lots of ¥500, ¥100, ¥50 coins |
| Compact umbrella or rain jacket | Spring showers are common |
| Comfortable walking shoes | Expect 15,000–25,000 steps/day |
| Motion sickness meds | For kids on the Shinkansen or winding mountain roads |
| Reusable water bottle | Japan has clean tap water everywhere |
| Power adapter | Not needed — Japan uses Type A plugs (same as U.S.) |

## 🌸 Cherry Blossoms

**Will we see cherry blossoms?**
**Yes — your timing is excellent.**
- **Tokyo** estimated peak: March 26 – April 3 (you arrive March 28)
- **Osaka** estimated peak: April 2 – 10 (you're there April 2–4)

**Best spots to see blossoms on this trip:**
- **Tokyo:** Ueno Park, Sumida River walk (near Asakusa), Shinjuku Gyoen
- **Osaka:** Osaka Castle Park (Nishinomaru Garden, open until 9 PM during season), Kema Sakuranomiya Park

Bring a picnic blanket, grab bento boxes from a convenience store, and enjoy hanami (flower viewing) under the trees. Evening illuminations are especially beautiful.

## 📱 Mobile Connectivity

**What's the best way to stay connected in Japan?**
The group plans to use **Verizon TravelPass** ($10/day per line, charged only on days used). Confirm it's enabled on each line before departure. It works in both Taiwan and Japan.

Alternatives if you prefer local data:
- **eSIM** (e.g., Airalo) — buy online before you leave, activate on arrival
- **Pocket WiFi** — rent at the airport; shares one connection across the group
- **Local SIM** — available at airports; data-only (no calls)

## 🍽️ Food & Dietary Needs

**How do we handle food allergies in Japan?**
Print or save an allergy card in Japanese listing your restrictions — many restaurants will take this seriously. Apps like [Allergy Translation](https://www.allergyeats.com) can help. Avoid assumptions: Japanese dishes often contain hidden dashi (fish broth), soy, sesame, or shellfish.

**Are there vegetarian or halal options?**
Vegetarian options exist but require research — many soups use fish-based broth even without visible meat. Look for shojin ryori (Buddhist vegetarian cuisine) at temple restaurants. Halal-certified restaurants are available in Tokyo and Osaka; search [Japan Halal](https://www.halalmedia.jp) for a directory.

**Can kids be picky eaters and still find food?**
Absolutely. Ramen, sushi, tempura, yakitori, and Japanese curry are all kid-approved. Convenience stores (7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson) have a surprisingly high-quality selection of onigiri, sandwiches, hot foods, and snacks — a reliable fallback for picky moments.

## 🛡️ Safety

**Is Japan safe for families?**
Japan consistently ranks among the safest countries in the world. Petty crime is rare, streets are clean, and locals are helpful. The main risks are practical: getting lost, overcrowded train platforms during rush hour, and walking fatigue. Kids can move around freely in most tourist areas.

**Any areas or situations to be cautious about?**
- **Rush hour trains (7–9 AM, 5–8 PM)** — very crowded; hold hands with kids
- **Osaka's Dotonbori at night** — lively and safe, but very crowded and loud; keep the group together
- **Pickpockets** — extremely rare in Japan, but stay aware in very crowded areas like Shibuya Crossing

## 🙏 Tipping

**Do we tip in Japan?**
**No — never tip in Japan.** Tipping is not part of the culture and can be confusing or even considered rude. Service is a point of professional pride and is built into the price. This applies to restaurants, taxis, hotels, and tour guides.

**What about Taiwan?**
Tipping is also not expected in Taiwan. Some upscale restaurants include a 10% service charge automatically.
