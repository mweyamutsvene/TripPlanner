# Cultural Notes – Taiwan 🇹🇼 & Japan 🇯🇵

Prepared for U.S.-based travelers. These notes cover etiquette, customs, and practical tips that differ from what you're used to at home.

---

## 🇹🇼 TAIWAN (March 28–30)

### Etiquette Essentials
- **Greetings:** A slight nod or handshake is fine. No bowing required (unlike Japan).
- **Shoes:** Remove shoes when entering someone's home or an Airbnb. Most Taiwanese homes have indoor slippers near the entrance — use them.
- **Temples:** Dress modestly (cover shoulders/knees). Don't point at statues. Don't step on the threshold when entering.
- **Chopstick rules:** Don't stick chopsticks upright in rice (resembles incense for the dead). Don't pass food chopstick-to-chopstick.

### Tipping
- **No tipping culture.** Tipping is not expected at restaurants, taxis, or hotels. Some upscale restaurants include a 10% service charge automatically.

### Cash vs Card
- **Cash is king** at night markets, small shops, and street food stalls.
- **EasyCard** (transit card) is essential — works on MRT, buses, convenience stores, and some taxis. Buy one at any MRT station (~NT$100 / ~$3 USD for the card, then load money).
- Most 7-Eleven and FamilyMart locations have ATMs that accept international cards.
- Credit cards accepted at malls, larger restaurants, and department stores.

- English is not widely spoken outside tourist areas, but the younger generation and MRT signage are bilingual.
- Google Translate camera mode works well for menus.

### Night Market Tips
- **Ningxia Night Market** (your Day 1 evening): Smaller, food-focused, less overwhelming than Shilin. Great for kids.
- Expect to pay NT$50–150 ($1.50–$5 USD) per dish.
- Bring wet wipes / hand sanitizer — no handwashing stations at stalls.
- Don't be shy about pointing at what you want if you can't read the menu.

### Common U.S. Tourist Mistakes in Taiwan
- Forgetting cash for night markets (ATM first!)
- Over-planning: Taipei is walkable and the MRT is excellent. Leave room to wander.
- Not trying bubble tea from a local shop (it was invented here!)

## 🇯🇵 JAPAN (March 30 – April 4)

### Etiquette Essentials
- **Bowing:** A slight bow (15°) when greeting, thanking, or apologizing. Don't overthink it — the attempt is appreciated.
- **Shoes OFF:** Remove shoes in temples, traditional restaurants, Airbnbs, many fitting rooms. Look for a shoe area or raised floor. Wear socks without holes.
- **Quiet voices:** Speak softly on trains and in public. No phone calls on trains. Set phones to silent ("manner mode").
- **Queuing:** The Japanese queue for everything. Don't cut lines. Stand on the left side of escalators (Tokyo) and right side (Osaka) to let people pass.
- **Trash:** There are almost no public trash cans. Carry a small bag for your garbage. Convenience stores have bins you can use.
- **Chopstick rules:** Same as Taiwan — don't stick them upright in rice, don't pass food chopstick-to-chopstick. Don't rub disposable chopsticks together (implies they're cheap/dirty).

### Tipping
- **NEVER TIP in Japan.** It can be considered rude or confusing. This applies to restaurants, taxis, hotels, and tour guides. Service is included in the price and considered a matter of professional pride.

### Cash vs Card
- **Japan is still heavily cash-based**, especially at small restaurants, ramen shops, temples, and street food stalls.
- Carry at least ¥10,000–20,000 ($65–130 USD) per day for your group.
- **7-Eleven ATMs** (7Bank) and **Japan Post ATMs** reliably accept U.S. debit/credit cards.
- IC Cards (**Suica** or **Pasmo**) for transit: Can be bought at train stations or loaded on Apple Wallet. Works on all Tokyo and Osaka trains/buses, plus convenience stores and vending machines.
- Major stores and chains accept credit cards, but don't count on it everywhere.

- Many signs in Tokyo and Osaka have English. Train announcements are bilingual.
- Google Translate camera mode is a lifesaver for restaurant menus.

### Restaurant Customs
- **No walking and eating.** Eat where you buy, especially at markets.
- Many restaurants have a **ticket vending machine** (食券機, shokkenki) at the entrance — buy your meal ticket before sitting down.
- Saying **"Itadakimasu"** (I humbly receive) before eating and **"Gochisousama"** (thank you for the meal) when done is polite.
- Slurping noodles is not only acceptable — it's a compliment.
- Water and tea are free at restaurants. Asking for extra water is fine.
- Some small restaurants don't have English menus — look for picture menus or plastic food models in the window.

### Temple & Shrine Etiquette
- **Temples (Buddhist):** Burn incense, ring the bell if available, toss a coin (¥5 is lucky), bow, clap, pray, bow.
- **Shrines (Shinto):** At the purification fountain, wash left hand, then right hand, rinse mouth (don't drink), then wash left hand again.
- Don't take photos of worshippers or in restricted areas.
- Senso-ji in Asakusa: Huge incense burner at the entrance — waft smoke over yourself for good health (the kids will love this).

### Trains & Transit
- **IC Cards (Suica/Pasmo):** Load one per person. Tap to enter, tap to exit. Works seamlessly.
- Children ages 6–11 get **half-price** child IC cards (purchase at a staffed ticket window with proof of age).
- **Priority seats** (marked in a different color): Give up for elderly, pregnant, disabled, or those with small children.
- **Don't eat on local trains** (it's fine on bullet trains/shinkansen).
- Google Maps works excellently for train navigation in Japan.

### Cherry Blossom Season 🌸
Your timing is **incredible:**
- **Tokyo** estimated peak: **March 26 – April 3** (you're there March 30 – April 2)
- **Osaka** estimated peak: **April 2 – 10** (you're there April 2 – 4)

Best cherry blossom spots along your route:
- **Tokyo:** Ueno Park, Sumida River walk (right near your Asakusa apartment!), Shinjuku Gyoen
- **Osaka:** Osaka Castle Park (Nishinomaru Garden — open until 9 PM during cherry blossom season!), Kema Sakuranomiya Park

Hanami (flower viewing) tips:
- Bring a picnic blanket for under the trees
- Convenience stores sell hanami bento boxes and snacks
- Best lighting is late afternoon and evening (many spots have nighttime illumination)
- It will be busy — arrive early for the best spots

### Common U.S. Tourist Mistakes in Japan
1. **Tipping** — Don't do it. Really.
2. **Being loud** — Americans tend to be louder than Japanese norms, especially on trains.
3. **Not carrying cash** — You'll get stuck at many restaurants and attractions.
4. **Blowing your nose in public** — Step away from people; use a bathroom if possible. Sniffling is preferred over blowing.
5. **Wearing shoes inside** — Check before entering. When in doubt, look at others.
6. **Assuming English** — Learn a few phrases. The effort is deeply appreciated.
7. **Jaywalking** — Wait for the signal, even if the road is empty. Everyone does.
8. **Not sorting trash** — Japan has strict recycling. Follow the labels on bins.

## Packing Essentials You Might Not Think Of

| Item | Why |
|------|-----|
| **Verizon TravelPass** | Already planned — $10/day per line, charged only on days used abroad. Confirm it's enabled before departure. Works in both Taiwan and Japan. |
| **Small towel/handkerchief** | Many restrooms in Japan don't have paper towels or hand dryers. Locals carry a small towel. |
| **Coin purse** | You'll accumulate LOTS of coins (¥500, ¥100, ¥50, ¥10). A coin purse helps. |
| **Comfortable walking shoes** | You'll walk 15,000–25,000 steps/day. Break them in before the trip. |
| **Light rain jacket / compact umbrella** | Late March / early April is rainy season transition. Pack layers. |
| **Power adapter** | Taiwan uses Type A/B (same as U.S.). Japan uses Type A (same as U.S. — no adapter needed!). Voltage is 100V in Japan (U.S. is 120V) — most modern chargers handle this fine. |
| **Plastic bags** | For carrying wet umbrellas and separating dirty laundry. |
| **Reusable water bottle** | Japan has clean tap water. Refill stations exist. |
| **Motion sickness meds** | For kids on the bullet train, buses, or Jiufen mountain roads. |
| **Ziploc bags** | Keep passports and electronics dry; store snacks for kids. |

## Visa & Entry Requirements (as of 2026)

### Taiwan
- **U.S. citizens:** Visa-free for stays up to 90 days.
- **Passport validity:** Must be valid for duration of stay.
- You'll clear immigration at TPE on arrival.

### Japan
- **U.S. citizens:** Visa-free for stays up to 90 days for tourism.
- **Visit Japan Web:** Pre-register your customs declaration and immigration info online before arrival. This speeds up entry at Narita. Do this 1-2 days before your March 30 arrival.
- **Passport validity:** Must be valid for duration of stay.
