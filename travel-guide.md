## 🇹🇼 TAIWAN TRANSIT (March 28–30)

### Taipei MRT (Subway)

The Taipei MRT is one of the easiest subway systems in the world. Everything is in English.

**How it works:**
1. **Tap in** with your EasyCard at the turnstile (blue reader pad on top of the gate)
2. Walk through
3. Follow signs to your line and direction (signs show the terminal station name for each direction)
4. Board the train
5. **Tap out** at your destination turnstile — the fare is deducted automatically

**Key facts:**
- Runs 6:00 AM – midnight
- Trains every 2–5 minutes during the day
- All announcements in Mandarin, Taiwanese, Hakka, and **English**
- All signs have English station names
- **No eating or drinking** on the MRT or in stations (this is strictly enforced — there are fines!)
- Air-conditioned and very clean

**Your key stations:**
| Destination | Station | Line |
|------------|---------|------|
| Your Airbnb (Q Square) | **Taipei Main Station** | All lines connect here |
| CKS Memorial Hall | **Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall** | Red or Green Line |
| Ningxia Night Market | **Zhongshan** (5 min walk) | Red Line |
| Daan Forest Park | **Daan Forest Park** | Red Line |
| Jiufen day trip | **Taipei Main Station** → train to Ruifang | Not MRT (see below) |

### EasyCard (Transit Card)

**What:** A rechargeable card that works on the MRT, buses, convenience stores, and many taxis.

**Where to buy:** Any MRT station — look for the add-value machines or the service counter.
- Card cost: NT$100 (~$3 USD) per card — non-refundable
- Load money on top (NT$300–500 per person is a good starting amount for 2 days)

**How to reload:**
1. Find any add-value machine in an MRT station (English interface available)
2. Place your card on the sensor
3. Insert cash (NT$100 bills or coins)
4. Done — balance updates automatically

**Who needs one:** All 5 of you. The 10-year-old's card is the same as everyone else's (no child card needed for MRT).

### Uber in Taipei

Works exactly like in the U.S. Same app, same interface.
- **Airport pickup (Day 1):** Open Uber at TPE airport after clearing baggage. Set destination to your Airbnb address. Estimated fare: NT$1,000–1,400 (~$30–45).
- **Airbnb drop-off instructions (Taxi or Uber):** Have the driver drop you off across from **69 Hwa-Yin Street, Da-Tong, TAIPEI** (台北市大同區華陰街69號). The host will meet you at the entrance at your agreed check-in time.
- Payment: Your U.S. credit card on file works.
- Drivers may call you in Mandarin — if this happens, text your location in the app's chat.

### Getting to Jiufen (Day 2 option)

Jiufen is a mountain town ~40 min east of Taipei. No MRT goes there.

**Route:**
1. **Taipei Main Station** → Take a **TRA train** (Taiwan Railway, NOT the MRT) to **Ruifang Station**
   - ~40 min, NT$76/person (~$2.40)
   - Use EasyCard to tap in at the TRA gates (separate from the MRT gates in the same station)
   - Look for signs to "TRA" (台鐵) — NOT "MRT" or "HSR"
2. **Ruifang Station** → Take **bus #788** or **#1062** to Jiufen Old Street
   - ~15 min, EasyCard works on the bus
   - Tap on when boarding, tap off when getting off
3. **Return:** Same route in reverse

> **Bus tip in Taiwan:** Enter through the front door. Some buses you tap your card when boarding, some when exiting, some both. Watch what the person ahead of you does, or look for the LED sign near the driver that says "上車刷卡" (tap when boarding) or "下車刷卡" (tap when exiting).

## 🇯🇵 JAPAN TRANSIT (March 30 – April 4)

Japan's train system is the best in the world, but it can be overwhelming at first. Here's exactly what you need to know.

### IC Cards (Suica / Pasmo) — Your Most Important Item

These are rechargeable cards that work on **all trains, subways, and buses** in Tokyo and Osaka. They also work at convenience stores, vending machines, and many restaurants.

**⚠️ Each person needs their OWN card or phone.** You cannot tap one phone multiple times to let everyone through — each family member walks through a separate gate and taps their own device or card at the same time.

**Where to get them:**

| Person | Card Type | Where to Get | How |
|--------|----------|-------------|-----|
| Adults (3) | Regular Suica | Apple Wallet on **their own iPhone** (recommended) or physical card from station kiosk | Each adult adds a Suica to their own phone's Wallet app. One Suica per phone. |
| 13-year-old | Regular **adult** Suica | Apple Wallet on **their own iPhone** if they have one, OR physical card from station kiosk | Age 12+ = adult fare in Japan. |
| 10-year-old | Regular **adult** Suica | Apple Wallet on their phone, OR physical card from station kiosk | Using an adult card to save time. Pays adult fare (~$7–13 more over the trip) but avoids the hassle of finding a staffed ticket window with a passport. |

> **How many phones do you need?** One per person using Apple Wallet Suica. Anyone without an iPhone buys a physical adult card at a station kiosk (¥500 deposit, refundable) — no staff interaction needed.

**Apple Wallet Suica (one per iPhone):**
- Works on any iPhone 8 or newer
- On each person's phone: Wallet app → "+" → Transit Card → Suica
- Load ¥2,000–3,000 to start (~$13–20)
- Just hold your phone near the reader at the gate — same as Apple Pay
- Reload anytime in the Wallet app with your credit card
- No deposit, no physical card to lose

**How to reload a physical card:**
1. Find a fare-adjustment / recharge machine in any station (look for machines with "チャージ" or "Charge")
2. Insert the card
3. Select the amount (¥1,000, ¥2,000, ¥3,000, etc.)
4. Insert cash
5. Take the card back

**How much to load:** Start with ¥2,000–3,000 per person. A typical day of trains in Tokyo costs ¥500–1,000 per person. You'll reload as needed.

### How to Ride a Train in Japan — Step by Step

This works the same for JR, Metro, and private railways:

**Step 1: Find your route**
- Open Google Maps, enter your destination, and tap "Directions" → transit icon (🚆)
- It will show you: which line, which direction, how many stops, where to transfer, total time, and cost
- **Pay attention to the line color and terminal station name** — these are how you navigate

**Step 2: Enter the station**
- Walk to any ticket gate
- **Tap your IC card** on the reader (right side of the gate, blue pad) — the gate opens
- If using a paper ticket instead: insert it face-up into the slot, walk through, grab the ticket from the slot on the other side
- The gate display shows your IC card balance

**Step 3: Find your platform**
- Follow overhead signs to your train line (signs are in Japanese AND English)
- Signs show the **line name** and the **terminal station** for each direction
- Example: "Asakusa Line → Nishi-Magome" means the Asakusa Line heading toward Nishi-Magome station
- Google Maps will tell you exactly which platform

**Step 4: Wait and board**
- Stand behind the floor markings in a line — trains stop with doors exactly at these marks
- Let everyone exit first, then board
- Overhead displays show the next train and its type (Local, Rapid, Express)

**Step 5: Ride**
- Announcements in Japanese and English name the next stop
- Electronic displays inside the train show the route and next station
- Keep voices low. No phone calls. Set phones to vibrate.

**Step 6: Exit**
- Get off at your stop
- Follow signs to the exit (改札 = ticket gate, 出口 = exit)
- **Tap your IC card** at the exit gate — fare is deducted automatically
- The display shows the fare deducted and your remaining balance

> **If your balance is too low:** The gate will beep and close. Don't panic — look for a fare-adjustment machine (精算機) near the gates. Insert your card, add money, and try the gate again.

### Transfers Between Lines

Many stations serve multiple train lines. Transferring is common and easy:

1. Google Maps will say something like: "Walk 3 min to transfer to Ginza Line"
2. Follow signs inside the station for your new line (color-coded)
3. **You do NOT tap out and back in** if it's a transfer within the same system (e.g., between two Metro lines)
4. **You DO tap out and back in** if transferring between different operators (e.g., Metro → JR). Google Maps will tell you when this happens — it shows two separate fare charges.

> **Tip:** Tokyo has multiple overlapping train operators (JR, Tokyo Metro, Toei Subway, private railways). It sounds confusing, but your IC card works on ALL of them. Just tap in, ride, tap out. The card handles the pricing automatically.

## 🚅 SHINKANSEN (BULLET TRAIN) — April 2

This is the big transit event of the trip: **Tokyo → Osaka** on the Nozomi Shinkansen.

### Before the Day: Book via SmartEX App
1. Download the **SmartEX** app and create an account (use your U.S. credit card)
2. Book **reserved seats** (指定席, shitei-seki) so your family sits together
3. Choose a **Nozomi** train — fastest and most frequent (every 10–15 min)
4. Select 5 seats together — aim for a row of 3 + row of 2 across the aisle
5. **Oversized luggage:** If any bag exceeds 160cm total (Length + Width + Height), you MUST reserve seats in the **last row** of a car (baggage storage behind the last row). No extra charge, but must be reserved.
6. Cost: ~¥14,720/adult × 4 (13yo = adult) + ¥7,360 × 1 (10yo = child) = ~¥66,240 (~$441 total)

### Day Of: Step-by-Step

**Getting to Tokyo Station:**
1. Check out of the Asakusa apartment
2. Take a **taxi** to Tokyo Station (recommended with luggage — ~20 min, ~¥2,500–3,500)
3. Alternatively: Asakusa Line subway to Nihombashi, walk to Tokyo Station (~25 min total), but dragging luggage through stations is tough

**At Tokyo Station:**
1. Enter through the regular ticket gates (tap IC card or use SmartEX QR/credit card)
2. Follow signs to **Shinkansen 新幹線** — these are clearly marked in English
3. Go through the **Shinkansen ticket gates** (a second set of gates, separate from regular ones)
   - **SmartEX:** Tap the credit card you registered in the app at the gate reader
   - The gate prints a small receipt showing your car and seat numbers
4. Find your **platform** — displays show departure times, train names, and car numbers
5. Floor markings on the platform show where each **car number** stops
6. Line up at your car number and wait

**On the Shinkansen:**
- Overhead racks for bags. Oversized bags go behind the last row (if reserved)
- Seat recline buttons on the armrest — OK to recline, but be gentle
- **Eating is not only fine — it's expected!** Buy an **ekiben** (train station bento box) at Tokyo Station before boarding. There are shops everywhere inside the gates.
- There's a restroom at the end of each car
- Free WiFi (JR Shinkansen WiFi) — connect, accept terms, 30 min sessions (reconnect as needed)
- The ride is whisper-quiet and smooth. The train hits 285 km/h (177 mph)
- Announcements in Japanese and English

**Arriving at Shin-Osaka:**
1. Exit through the Shinkansen gates (tap same credit card)
2. Then exit through the regular gates
3. Option A: Take the **Midosuji Line subway** to Namba (~15 min, ¥280) — follow signs to "Metro"
4. Option B: Take a **taxi** to your Namba/Nihonbashi Airbnb (~20 min, ~¥2,500–3,000)

> **Ekiben tip:** Tokyo Station has "Ekiben Matsuri" (駅弁屋 祭) — a shop with 200+ bento varieties from across Japan. It's inside the Shinkansen gates on the Yaesu side. Get one per person — it's a cultural experience. Budget ¥1,000–1,500 each.

## 🚕 TAXIS — When and How

### When to Use a Taxi
- Airport to/from Airbnb with luggage
- Late at night when trains stop (~midnight)
- Short hops when the whole family is tired
- Getting to Tokyo Station on Shinkansen day (with bags)

### How They Work
- **Doors open automatically** — the rear left door is operated by the driver. Do NOT try to open or close it yourself. Stand back and let it open.
- Tell the driver your destination. Best approach: **show your phone screen** with the destination in Google Maps (the Japanese address/name works best)
- Meters are standard — no negotiating. Tipping is not expected (and not wanted).
- Payment: Most taxis accept credit cards and IC cards now, but some still only take cash. Carry cash as backup.
- Starting fare: ~¥500 in Tokyo, ~¥680 in Osaka

### How to Catch One
- **Taxi stands** are at every station and major intersection — look for cars lined up
- The light on the roof or dashboard means **available** (red/orange characters 空車 = vacant)
- 🟢 Green light or 空車 sign = AVAILABLE
- 🔴 Red light or 賃走 sign = OCCUPIED
- You can also hail one on the street by raising your hand
- Uber works in some areas of Japan but regular taxis are usually easier and just as cheap

## 🆘 WHAT TO DO WHEN LOST OR CONFUSED

### On a Train
- **Went the wrong direction?** Get off at the next station, cross to the opposite platform, and go back. Your IC card handles the fare correctly.
- **Missed your stop?** Same thing — get off, go back. No penalty.
- **On the wrong line entirely?** Exit, re-enter, take the correct line. The IC card charges are based on distance, not mistakes.

### In a Station
- **Can't find your exit?** Look for overhead maps showing exit numbers and nearby landmarks. Or just pick any exit — you can walk around above ground and Google Maps will guide you.
- **Can't read the signs?** Station names are ALWAYS written in English (romaji) in Japan and Taiwan. Look up — the English is there.
- **Need help?** Look for a staffed ticket window (有人改札) or a person in a uniform. Station staff in Japan are incredibly helpful, even if they don't speak much English. Show them your destination on your phone.

### On the Street
- **Google Maps is your best friend.** As long as you have data (Verizon TravelPass), open Google Maps, tap the blue dot, and follow the walking directions.
- **Addresses in Japan work differently.** Buildings are numbered by construction order, not position on a street. This means #10 might be next to #23. Always use Google Maps — don't try to find addresses by patand.
- **Look for landmarks.** Convenience stores (7-Eleven, Lawson, FamilyMart) are on virtually every block and can serve as reference points.
- **Ask at a convenience store.** Staff are used to tourists. Show them your destination on your phone.
- **Police boxes (交番, kōban)** — small police stations on many street corners in Japan. Officers are friendly and will help you find your way. They often have area maps.

### Emergency Numbers
| Country | Emergency | Police | Ambulance |
|---------|----------|--------|-----------|
| Taiwan | 119 (fire/ambulance), 110 (police) | 110 | 119 |
| Japan | 110 (police), 119 (fire/ambulance) | 110 | 119 |

- U.S. Embassy Tokyo: 03-3224-5000
- U.S. Embassy Taipei (AIT): 02-2162-2000

### 7-Eleven ATMs in Japan
These are the most reliable ATMs for U.S. cards:
1. Walk into any 7-Eleven (there's one every few blocks)
2. The ATM is usually near the back
3. Press "English" on the screen
4. Insert your U.S. debit card
5. Select "Withdrawal"
6. Choose amount (¥10,000 increments)
7. Take cash and card
- **Hours:** Most 7-Eleven ATMs operate 24/7
- **Fees:** Your bank may charge a foreign transaction fee. 7Bank itself charges ¥110 per withdrawal.

**Taiwan:**

- No eating/drinking on the MRT or in stations.

**Japan:**
- Subway/train = tap IC card in, tap out. Works on ALL lines.
- Google Maps for all transit directions — trust it.
- Shinkansen requires a separate ticket (SmartEX app). Do NOT just tap your IC card.
- Taxis: Door opens automatically. Show destination on phone. No tipping.
- Cash still king at small restaurants. Always carry ¥10,000+.
- 7-Eleven ATMs accept U.S. cards.
- Stand left on escalators in Tokyo, right in Osaka.
- No phone calls on trains. Keep voices low.
- No eating on local trains (OK on Shinkansen).

**If lost:** Open Google Maps. Find a 7-Eleven. Ask station staff. Show your destination on your phone.

**If your IC card balance is too low at the gate:** Don't force through. Find the fare adjustment machine (精算機) near the gates, add money, then try again.
